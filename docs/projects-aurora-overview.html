SOLAR CAR ENERGY BALANCE

MOTIVATING PROBLEM

The Aurora Project was started in the wake of the 2023 World Solar Challenge with the intent of making a hollistcally better solar vehicle compared to its predecessor. The World Solar Challenge, a biennial 3022km race across Australia from Darwin to Adelaide prompts teams to construct highly efficient vehicles powered entirely by the sun. After competing in 2023 with Borealis, The Aurora Project represented a reconstiution of how a solar car is designed by the Blue Sky team. This page serves to describe the mission objectives and design process of Aurora.

While Borealis' construction was robust, the vehicle faced several challenges, including but not limited to:

  • Vehicle Weight: Fully loaded, the car came in at 315 kg: our competitors were averaging below 260 kg in our class.
  • Poor Weight Distribution: a 33.9% higher CGz compared to the intended design led to diffiuclt handling characteristics.
  • Errors in Tolerancing: A misalignment between the top deck and bottom shell left a large seam at the tail, leading to aero losses.

For our next race at the 2025 Formula Sun Grand Prix, we needed to improve the fidelity between the design and final product.

We also knew we could build a better car: lighter, faster, more efficient.

We also knew we could build a better car: lighter, faster, more efficient. But what are the vehicle parameters to prioritize, and how fast could the car drive (especially on a tight budget)? Over 2023, we obsereved many teams with 3-wheel cars, thus what are the tangible advantages/disadvantges of such design? A three wheel design pushes the trackwidth further out and widens the aeroshell, so would there be a significant aerodynamic penalty? We needed a way to compare potential solutions early on in the conceptual design process.

As Team Manager I was heavily involved at every level of the team's operation, from the design, production, and operation of the vehicle, to the logistics of going to competitions and securing partnerships to fund the team's missions.

TIME TO RACE

This vehicle was the first to be built on the principle of Race Time: The theoretical minutes it would take to finish a 3022 km race using a simplified energy balance around the solar car. Compared to previous measures of performance, this objectively quantified the trade-offs in vehicle perforamnce as a funciton of 17 different parameters. In other words, the less time it took to expend all of the energy collected throughout the race (ie, Energy In = Energy Out ), the faster the car could finish the race given a fixed distance.

Hence, I developed an Excel-based Solver to quickly estimate race time on each iteration of design. Of course, models like these can't consider vehicle dynamics. Maximising cornering velocities around a track would be critical to retain a top speed during track races like the Formula Sun Grand Prix, which we intended to compete at in 2025.

Since the greater arc of this project was to refine our fabrication practices and make incremental improvements across the board, we developed the following performance objectives we set Borealis as the benchmark:

  1. Finish Top 2 in the Formula Sun Grand Prix 2025 with a comparable race time of -Δ120 minutes.
  2. Lower total weight by at least 10%, with strict adherance to the master CAD assembly.
  3. Minimum 15% increase in cornering velocity.
